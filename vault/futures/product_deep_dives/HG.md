# [[HG]] — COMEX Copper

## Contract specs

- Exchange / product code: COMEX (CME Group) / HG
- Tick size / tick value: $0.0001 per lb = $2.50 per tick (micro: $0.00001 = $0.25 per tick on MGC)
- Contract months (delivery cycle): All 12 months; Jan, Mar, May, Jul, Sep, Dec most liquid (quarterly cycle)
- Session hours (RTH and extended): Sunday–Friday, 5:00 PM–4:00 PM CT (nearly continuous, with 60-min gap 4:00–5:00 PM)
- First notice / last trading day: 2nd to last business day of contract month
- Settlement: Physical delivery (25,000 lbs of cathode copper, 99.99% purity)
- Margin (initial / maintenance at Topstep, if known): Typically $2.5–4k initial, $2–3.5k maint on standard HG; tighter on MGC (micro)

## What it actually is

Copper (HG) is the COMEX industrial metals contract representing 25,000 pounds of high-purity copper cathode. Unlike precious metals, copper is almost purely an industrial commodity—it is the lifeblood of modern electronics, power transmission, construction, and renewable energy infrastructure. There is negligible monetary (store-of-value) demand. This makes copper exquisitely sensitive to economic cycles, industrial production, and expectations around growth. The phrase "Dr. Copper" exists because copper's price movements often correctly diagnose economic health before equities or bonds do. Hedgers include mines (selling forward), smelters (buying input), and end-users (utilities, auto, construction); speculators range from macro traders to commodity indices to Chinese state-owned enterprises.

## Primary drivers

Ranked by influence in the current regime (Apr 2026—watch China stimulus rollout, Fed rate trajectory, US construction data, energy-transition capex):

1. **Economic growth / industrial cycle (PMI, ISM, Chinese property)** — The #1 driver. Copper is a leading indicator of GDP. Surprise in US ISM manufacturing > 50 (expansion) tends to lift copper 1–2% within 2 trading days. Chinese Caixin Manufacturing PMI on 1st business day of month is the single most important print for copper. Property-sector weakness in China directly suppresses demand (20–30% of global use).
2. **USD strength (DXY)** — Inverse relationship (r ≈ −0.60). Strong USD (> 105) suppresses copper prices because end-users in EM countries pay more in local currency and demand falls. Weak USD (< 100) supports copper on export competitiveness and EM purchasing power.
3. **Real interest rates (10Y TIPS yield)** — Moderately negative correlation (r ≈ −0.40). Higher real rates increase the cost of capital for mining capex and for end-user capex projects (utilities, builders), suppressing demand. Unlike gold, this is not the #1 driver—the industrial demand cycle dominates.
4. **Mining disruptions (labor, political, environmental)** — Secondary but volatile. Chile (28% of global supply), Peru (11%), Indonesia (10%), Zambia. Labor strikes, environmental bans (Chile restricting water), or political risk (Chile copper taxes) can cause supply shocks and multiyear swings. Monitor closely.
5. **Energy-transition capex expectations** — Growing driver post-2020. EV battery adoption (one EV uses 2–3x copper vs ICE car), renewable grid buildout (wind, solar require massive copper for transmission), and electrification spending. Positive surprises in EV sales, utility capex, or IRA-style subsidies tend to lift copper 0.5–1.5% per event.
6. **Inventory levels (LME, SHFE registered stocks)** — LME registered copper often moves inverse to price. Low inventory (< 250k tonnes) signals physical tightness and supports prices on supply fears. High inventory (> 500k) signals weakness. SHFE bonded inventory in Shanghai is a secondary signal; rapid drawdowns suggest Chinese demand.

## Key correlations

- **Positively correlated with:**
  - [[ES]], [[NQ]], [[YM]] Equity indices (r ≈ +0.70–0.75). Copper and equities both respond to growth expectations. When equities rally on better-than-expected earnings, copper usually follows within 1–2 days. Correlation is highest during risk-on regimes; decouples in safe-haven crises.
  - [[CL]] Crude Oil (r ≈ +0.50). Both commodities respond to economic activity and EM demand. Oil is slightly more sensitive to supply shocks; copper more to growth shocks.
  - [[ZC]], [[ZS]] Corn/Soybeans (r ≈ +0.35–0.45). Softer correlation, but all commodities tend to co-move in broad risk-on/risk-off cycles.
  - Chinese equity indices and property stocks (indirect but important). Weakness in HSCEI or CSI property index often precedes copper weakness by 1–2 sessions.
  - Real breakevens (5Y5Y inflation expectations, r ≈ +0.35). Copper is inflation-sensitive but only if tied to growth (cost-push inflation is bearish; demand-pull inflation is bullish).

- **Negatively correlated with:**
  - [[DXY]] Dollar index (r ≈ −0.60). This is the most reliable tactical correlation. DXY spike = copper weakness, especially on USD safe-haven flows.
  - [[ZB]], [[ZF]] T-Bonds, nominal yields (r ≈ −0.45). Higher nominal rates suppress growth expectations, which suppresses copper.
  - [[DGS10]] TIPS real yields (r ≈ −0.40). Weaker than gold/silver, because the growth cycle dominates over the carry/real-rate story.
  - Volatility spikes / risk-off (VIX > 25, r ≈ −0.55). Safe-haven flows into USD and Treasuries pull capital from commodities.

- **Lead/lag relationships:**
  - Economic surprise indices (Citi Economic Surprise Index) lead copper by 3–7 trading days. Positive economic surprises tend to precede copper rallies.
  - Chinese PMI leads copper by 1–3 days; this is the most reliable lead signal.
  - Copper often leads equities by 1–2 hours on Fed decisions or inflation prints (it reprices growth/inflation expectations faster than equity index components).
  - Mining production disruptions are idiosyncratic; when announced (usually on news), copper spikes within 30 min.

## Recurring patterns

- **Seasonal (calendar effects):**
  - Jan–Feb: Winter production disruptions in Chile (Southern Hemisphere summer, environmental activism peaks). Potential for supply-shock rallies. Chinese New Year in late Jan/early Feb creates trading gaps.
  - Mar–May: Northern Hemisphere spring; construction season ramps. Seasonal pickup in copper demand (wiring, infrastructure). Copper often outperforms equities here.
  - Jun–Jul: Summer doldrums + potential for miner maintenance (Northern Hemisphere winter shutdown in logistics). May see consolidation or weakness if growth fears pick up.
  - Aug–Sep: Back-to-school capex + utility summer AC load preparation (electricity demand). Modest strength. FOMC decision typically in Sep; watch rate expectations.
  - Oct–Nov: Q4 earnings expectations ramp; if optimistic, copper rallies. Harvest-season weakness in agricultural inputs (fertilizer, irrigation equipment) may limit gains.
  - Dec: Year-end rebalancing. Often weak on fund de-risking, but strong if tech capex expectations spike (data centers, AI infra). Watch holiday demand surprises.

- **Event-driven:**
  - Chinese PMI (Caixin Manufacturing, NBS Manufacturing) — 1st business day of month. Surprise above 50 is bullish; below 50 is bearish. Impact can be ±1% same-day.
  - US ISM Manufacturing and ISM Non-Manufacturing — release at 10 AM CT on first business day of month and third Friday, respectively. Surprise above 50 is bullish for copper; below 50 is bearish.
  - FOMC decisions and Fed communication — rate hike expectations impact real rates and growth sentiment. A pivot to rate cuts is usually bullish; a hawkish surprise is bearish.
  - US construction spending and housing starts — released monthly. Strength is bullish; weakness is bearish. Copper is construction's #1 metal.
  - Chilean and Peruvian political developments and labor news — watch for strike threats, policy changes (copper tax increases), or environmental restrictions. These can push copper ±2–3% in hours.
  - Mining company earnings and production guidance — when large miners (Freeport-McMoRan, BHP, Rio Tinto) report, watch for production cuts or capex surprises. Positive surprises (output growth) can push copper down (supply easing); negative surprises (production cuts) can push up.
  - EV sales and battery production reports — increasingly important. Positive EV surprises (Tesla, BYD, legacy auto EV launches) are now material to copper (each EV = 100+ lbs of copper vs 45 lbs in ICE car).

- **Time-of-day patterns:**
  - London open (12:00 PM UTC = 6:00 AM CT): Modest volume spike; European equity selling can pressure copper.
  - London close / NY open (4:00 PM UTC = 10:00 AM CT): US data often drops here. Volatility peak, especially if PMI or ISM data.
  - Asian close (9:00 PM CT to 11:00 PM CT equivalent to Shanghai/Tokyo closes): Often sees post-Asian-market moves (Chinese investors repricing after mainland close).
  - US close (2:00 PM CT): Quieter; consolidation and book-squaring.
  - Overnight (5:00 PM–10:00 PM CT): Lower volume, wider spreads; overnight moves on Asian/European developments.

- **Calendar quirks:**
  - Roll windows (6–10 days before first notice): Front month can decouple on carry arbitrage. Vol can spike; spreads widen.
  - SHFE (Chinese domestic copper) opens 5 PM CT and closes 3 AM CT (next morning). SHFE copper moves often lead COMEX overnight 5 PM–8 PM CT by 10–20 min. Monitor real-time if trading overnight.
  - Month-end (last 2 days of contract): Technical unwinding; fund rebalancing. Often weaker.
  - Delivery month: As first notice day approaches, basis can spike. Front month can disconnect from back months.

## Common setups

1. **Economic surprise breakout:**
   - Trigger: Chinese PMI or US ISM prints above 52 (strong expansion) after being in 48–50 range (borderline). Copper consolidates 3–5 days; then break above on the beat.
   - Invalidation: Next week's economic data disappoints, or DXY spikes > 1%. Real rates spike on Fed hawkishness.
   - Exit: First target 1.5–2% above entry; second target 4%. Trail a stop at 5-day low.
   - Win rate: ~60% when economic trend is clearly positive. ~45% in choppy/mixed data regime.

2. **Chinese property / stimulus reversal:**
   - Trigger: China announces stimulus measures (credit expansion, property support) or property-sector stabilization news. Copper rallies 1–3% on anticipation of construction demand.
   - Invalidation: Stimulus is weaker-than-expected or doesn't flow to construction; Chinese equity indices fall despite stimulus news.
   - Exit: Trail stop at 3% profit or hold for 5–7 day target. Exit on DXY surge (safe-haven unwind of stimulus).
   - Win rate: ~55–58% depending on stimulus credibility.

3. **Supply-shock squeeze:**
   - Trigger: Large mine disruption announced (strike, environmental shutdown, geopolitical). Copper gaps up 1–2%. LME inventory starts declining. Momentum traders chase.
   - Invalidation: Supply disruption is resolved faster than expected, or underlying demand is weak (makes supply disruption irrelevant).
   - Exit: Take 50% at +3%; trail the rest at a 2% stop. Avoid holding through resolution news.
   - Win rate: ~65% on true supply shocks (labor wins, political bans). ~50% on speculative disruption fears that resolve.

4. **DXY weakness / USD carry unwind:**
   - Trigger: Fed pivots to rate-cut expectations (or actual cuts), DXY breaks below 102. Copper rallies 1–2% over 2–3 days. Often accompanied by EM currency strength.
   - Invalidation: Geopolitical risk flares (war, trade shock), causing safe-haven USD bid that overwhelms carry unwind.
   - Exit: Trail stop at 2–3 below entry, or take profit at +2.5% if DXY stabilizes.
   - Win rate: ~58% when the dollar move is structural (Fed pivot), ~48% when it's a false signal (bounce).

5. **Mean reversion after crash:**
   - Trigger: Copper sells off 7–10% on recession fears or growth shock (e.g., China surprise weakness). Oversold RSI < 30. Then positive surprise (PMI beat, stimulus, Fed easing) causes rebound.
   - Invalidation: Economic deterioration accelerates; recession is confirmed; DXY continues to rally.
   - Exit: Trail a stop at recent low + 1%. First target 50% of lost ground; second target full recovery. Often takes 5–10 days.
   - Win rate: ~62% in counter-trend bounces after genuine shocks. ~50% if recession is real (no rebound).

## Classic traps

- **Growth-sensitivity misread:** Traders assume copper and equities move together (they do, r ≈ +0.70–75). But in "stagflation" scenarios (growth falls, inflation rises), copper and equities diverge. Equities fall on lower profit expectations; copper can stay flat or rise on inflation expectations. Know the regime.
- **China stimulus hope-fade:** China announces stimulus → copper rallies 2–3% on 10M oz of demand hope. But if stimulus doesn't flow to construction capex (e.g., goes to financial asset support instead), copper fades over 2–3 weeks. Stimulus announcements are traps 40% of the time.
- **Mining supply fatigue:** A mine shutdown or strike can cause a 2–3% spike in copper. But if underlying demand is weak (PMI < 48), the supply shock is a short-squeeze that reverses hard. Always check demand before chasing supply-shock rallies.
- **Inventory builds on weakness:** Sometimes copper falls on weak demand, but LME inventory levels are ambiguous (may be temporary financing by producers, not true oversupply). Shorting copper based on rising inventory alone is a trap. Need to confirm with demand data.
- **Real-rate sensitivity overestimation:** Traders think copper = precious metals + growth. Real rates spike → copper down. But copper is weaker to real rates than gold/silver (r ≈ −0.40 vs −0.65 for gold). If real rates spike but growth expectations are stable, copper barely moves. Growth cycle is king.
- **Overnight gap volatility:** COMEX copper has a 60-min close (4 PM–5 PM CT); any Asian news (Chinese property announcements, SHFE closes) can gap copper 0.5–1.5% when RTH opens at 5 PM CT. Large holdings overnight are risky.
- **Concentration risk in mining supply:** Chile, Peru, Indonesia supply 50%+ of copper. Any one geopolitical shock (Boric threatens to raise taxes, Castillo in Peru faces unrest) can cascade. Traders often ignore Chile risk; it hits hard when it hits.

## Liquidity profile

- **Average daily volume (front month):** ~180–250k contracts (equivalent to ~4.5–6.25M tonnes per day in notional; actual global production is ~20M tonnes/year, so this is highly liquid). One of COMEX's top 3 most liquid commodities.
- **Open interest trend:** Typically 200–350k contracts open (varies by season and macro regime). Jan, Mar, May, Jul, Sep, Dec are the liquid months; others are thin. Heavy spec positioning during risk-on periods; long liquidation during risk-off.
- **Pre-open / post-close behavior:** 5:00 PM CT Sunday open sees 10–20% of daily vol in first 30 min (carryover from Friday close + weekend Asia news). Asian news (SHFE copper moves, Chinese economic surprises) often hit overnight 5 PM–8 PM CT, causing gap fills at next RTH open (10 AM CT).
- **Session with best fills:** 9:30 AM–12:00 PM CT (US morning, overlapping London, before lunch). Spreads tighten to $0.0005–0.001 per lb. Avoid 4:00 PM–5:00 PM CT (the close gap) for size.
- **Week with best liquidity:** Tuesday–Thursday; Monday and Friday see thinner volume.

## Options (if applicable)

- **Monthly / quarterly expirations:**
  - Monthly options (American-style) expire 4 Fridays before contract delivery month (same as most COMEX contracts).
  - Quarterly options (Jan, Apr, Jul, Oct) used by large hedgers (miners, utilities) for longer-term production cost hedges.

- **AM vs PM settlement:** COMEX HG options are PM-settled; settlement value is set at 2:00 PM CT (RTH close).

- **Typical IV rank range:** 30–75 depending on regime. Baseline ~45–50. Spikes to 60–75 during FOMC days, China stimulus surprises, or mining disruptions. Crashes to 20–30 during summer doldrums or stable growth periods.

- **Pin-risk behavior:** HG has pin-risk around round-number strike clusters (e.g., $3.00, $3.50 per lb). Avoid shorting calls or buying puts within $0.10 of major strikes in final 3 days of contract month. Large miners and utilities have substantial put positions; pin behavior can be violent.

## Risk notes

- **Gap risk profile:** MODERATE to HIGH overnight. Typical gap on SHFE copper move (Chinese market close flow to COMEX open): 0.5–1.2%. China surprise (stimulus, property shock): 1.5–3%. Ukraine-scale geopolitical shock: 3–5%. *Always* use a stop-loss—no overnight naked longs or shorts.
- **Limit-up / limit-down mechanics:** HG has a 10% daily limit (expanded to 15% during extreme vol). Limit-up or limit-down scenarios occur roughly 2–3 times per year on supply shocks (major mine closure) or geopolitical events. Trading halts 15 min, then limit expands and resets nightly.
- **Worst weekly move in last 5 years:** –14.5% (week of March 16–20, 2020, COVID crash). +10.2% (week of June 25–29, 2022, China stimulus hope). Expect ±3–5% in normal weeks; ±10% in high-beta macro weeks; ±15% possible in geopolitical/supply shock weeks.
- **Tail-risk events to remember:**
  - COVID crash (March 2020): Copper fell 13% in 3 trading days on recession fears and demand collapse.
  - China stimulus (Nov 2023): Copper rallied 11% over 2 weeks on hopes for property-sector recovery.
  - Chile political risk (2021–2023): Multiple 2–4% swings on tax and environmental headlines from the Boric administration; finally stabilized after negotiations.
  - Energy crisis (2022): Copper rallied 8% in the fall as inflation fears spiked; then fell 7% as Fed hawkishness reasserted.
  - US banking stress (March 2023): Copper fell 5% intraday on financial-crisis fears, then recovered quickly as Fed contained the crisis.
